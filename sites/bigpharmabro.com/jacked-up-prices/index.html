Pharmaceuticals and health products (not including insurance) make up the most profitable industry sector in the world. 1 Unlike in many other commercial industries, the price drug corporations charge for any given drug has nothing to do with the cost of its production, including the cost of clinical trials. Nor do drug prices have anything to do with how effective they are. 2 Rather, because the pharma industry is selling its products almost exclusively to private insurance companies and state-run health programs in countries like England or Norway, drug prices are based on a single priority: charge each buyer as much as they can possibly pay, while ensuring that no country or insurance provider pays so little that other countries or providers demand the same affordable price. 2, 5

Because Medicare, the U.S.â€™s largest single purchaser of pharma products, is prohibited from negotiating lower drug prices with manufacturers, the prices we see in the U.S. arenâ€™t based on how many tax dollars we can spare for Medicare, but how many dollars private insurance companies can afford to hand over. 2, 4, 5 Even with the discounts some insurance companies negotiate, drug prices in the U.S. are higher than those in other wealthy countries 7 times out of 8. 5
